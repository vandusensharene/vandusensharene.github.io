Southern Water Corp — Production Economics

An economic and statistical analysis of a desalination utility's three production plants: cost-to-produce vs. volume relationships, economies of scale, plant-level correlation analysis, and Python-based statistical work on pump failure behavior using rolling standard deviation and regression.

// Key Findings

What the data showed

A clear negative cost-volume relationship exists at every plant. As production volume rises, cost per megaliter falls — the classic economies-of-scale pattern driven by fixed costs spreading over larger output. The effect is strongest at Kootha (r = −0.83) and moderate at Surjek and Jutik (r ≈ −0.50).

Surjek is the operational outlier. Its cost band ranges from $32.61 to $86.30 per megaliter with the weakest scale benefits relative to its size, and a pronounced cost spike in November 2013 — the single biggest opportunity for operational review in the network.

The statistics unit extended the analysis to equipment reliability. Using Python, rolling standard deviation on pump sensor variables isolated the volatility signature that precedes pump failure events, and a multivariate regression identified which operating variables carry the most predictive weight.

// Recommendations

Management takeaways

Prioritize volume at Kootha

Steepest cost-reduction curve in the network — every additional gigaliter routed here earns the largest unit-cost benefit.

Investigate Surjek's cost variability

Audit the November 2013 anomaly and establish minimum viable production thresholds to keep the plant inside its efficient operating band.

Use the volume-cost curves for planning

Forecast production costs from planned volumes, schedule maintenance in low-demand windows, and balance load across plants using each plant's actual scale economics.
